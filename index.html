About Dream Cartography

Through Dream Cartography we strive for the modeling, documenting, and graphically depicting the dream space and the relevant psychological aspects of dreams in a meaningful but abstracted way. Our plan is to bring cartography and psychology together and get new insights, through cartography, into the subject of dreams. Cartography serves here as an instrument that allows explorative data analysis, by offering valuable and aesthetic visualizations.

The memory of a dream tends to disappear, if no description of it is made shortly after the dream experience. Therefore, for studying and understanding dreams, we need at least a dream description. But graphical representations of a dream can add clarity to its description, since the spatial representation facilitates grasping and comparing data. This helps in equal measure dream researches, psychotherapists and persons interested in their dreams to establish correspondences between dream space and other dream components.

The dream space is fundamentally different from the real space, especially because one cannot consciously visit dream place again, to survey it and account for missing information. From our point of view, this poses a cartographic challenge, which is enhanced by the setting construction in dreams. A dream place may be defined geographically, but may be also a mixed setting (where features of more than one place, real or fictive, are combined), condensed (as mixed, but embedding also personal experiences and thoughts), distorted (where the spatial proportions are different than in reality), uncertain or totally invented settings. For the visualization of the dream settings we desire to use all dream elements which are related to space, probably along to some predefined schemas and symbols, which can enhance the geographical information named as such (and visualized using open geodata, such as openstreetmaps). Depending on the characteristics of the dream settings, we may also use methods for uncertainty visualization.

We cordially invite you to upload a dream and create a map for it using the tools provided in the portal Do-It-Yourself Dream Map (experimental version). Please come back soon and check the updates of the mapping platform, edit a dream map (using the URL with the dream id), check for new dreams and new dream maps, for new pictures. Please consider that this is work in progress (last update: July 2018).

The research work is performed in the frame of an ambitious dissertation project at the ETH Zurich, Institute of Cartography and Geoinformation (IKG), Chair of Cartography (Prof. Lorenz Hurni). Cristina M. Iosifescu Enescu started her PhD in January 2015 and is expected to promovate in the beginning of 2019.
